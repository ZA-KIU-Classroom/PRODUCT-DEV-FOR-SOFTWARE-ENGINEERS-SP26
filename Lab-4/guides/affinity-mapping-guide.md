# Affinity Mapping Guide

**Time required:** 40 minutes  
**Materials:** Sticky notes (physical or Miro), markers, wall or table space  
**Team:** Full team participates. One person should not do this alone.

---

## What Is Affinity Mapping?

Affinity mapping is a technique for organizing large amounts of qualitative data into meaningful groups. You take individual insights from your interviews and physically group them by similarity until patterns emerge naturally.

The key principle: let the data organize itself. Do not start with categories and sort into them. Start with individual notes and see what clusters appear.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Extract Insights (15 minutes)

Go through every interview log and write one insight per sticky note. An insight is any of these:

- A verbatim quote that reveals a pain, need, or behavior
- A workaround or coping mechanism the user described
- A frustration, desire, or emotional reaction
- A behavior pattern (what they do, when they do it, why)
- A surprising or contradictory finding

**Rules for extraction:**
- One insight per sticky note. Do not combine multiple ideas on one note.
- Use the interviewee's language, not your interpretation.
- Include the interview number on each note (e.g., "P03" in the corner).
- Aim for 5 to 8 notes per interview. With 10 interviews, that gives you 50 to 80 notes.
- Every team member should extract from at least 2 to 3 interviews.

**What a good sticky note looks like:**
- "Wasted 30 min walking between buildings looking for a study room" (P03)
- "I just text my friend to ask if the library is full" (P07)
- "I feel embarrassed when I show up and there is no space" (P02)

**What a bad sticky note looks like:**
- "Users want a better experience" (too vague, whose words are these?)
- "The app should have a map feature" (this is a solution, not an insight)
- "People are frustrated" (no specifics, no source)

---

### Step 2: Silent Sorting (10 minutes)

Spread all sticky notes on a wall or table. Now, without speaking, every team member starts grouping notes that seem related. Move notes next to other notes that feel similar.

**Rules for silent sorting:**
- No talking. This prevents one loud voice from dominating.
- Anyone can move any note. If someone moves a note you placed, let it go.
- If a note seems to belong in two groups, write a duplicate.
- Some notes will not fit anywhere. That is fine. Leave them as outliers.
- Do not create group labels yet. Just cluster by similarity.

This should feel messy. That is normal. Trust the process.

---

### Step 3: Label Clusters (10 minutes)

Now talk. As a team, look at each cluster and name it. The name should describe the theme, not just list the contents.

**Good cluster names:**
- "Time wasted on manual scheduling"
- "Trust deficit with existing tools"
- "Social pressure to find group spaces"

**Bad cluster names:**
- "Cluster 1" (meaningless)
- "Problems" (too broad)
- "App features" (this is about solutions, not insights)

Aim for 5 to 8 clusters. If you have more than 10, some clusters are too granular and should be merged. If you have fewer than 4, you may not have extracted enough insights.

---

### Step 4: Document (5 minutes)

Take a clear photo of the entire board (physical) or export the Miro board as a high-resolution PNG.

Fill in the `affinity-map-template.md` with your cluster names, sample insights, and initial observations.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too few notes.** If you have fewer than 40, go back to your interview logs and extract more. You are leaving data on the table.
2. **Notes are too abstract.** "Users are unhappy" is not an insight. "P05 spent 45 minutes trying to find a quiet room and gave up" is an insight.
3. **One person does all the work.** Every team member must extract from at least 2 to 3 interviews. This is a team exercise.
4. **Clustering by interview instead of by theme.** Do not group all of P03's notes together. Group by what the note is about, not who said it.
5. **Skipping the silent sort.** Talking during sorting leads to premature consensus. Do the silent sort first, then discuss.
