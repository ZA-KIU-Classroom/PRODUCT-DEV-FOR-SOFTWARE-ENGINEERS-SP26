# Example: Competitive Landscape Seed

**Team:** StudySpace Finders  
**Date:** April 3, 2026  
**Purpose:** All current solutions, workarounds, and alternatives mentioned by interviewees.

---

## Direct Competitors

| Solution | Type | Mentioned By | What Users Said | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|----------|------|-------------|----------------|-----------|------------|
| University room booking portal | Web app | P01, P04, P06 | "It exists but nobody uses it for study rooms. It is built for classes." | Official, covers all rooms | Requires 24-hour advance booking, clunky login, no walk-in support |
| Google Maps busy times | Feature | P08 | "I sometimes check if the library shows as busy on Google Maps." | Free, familiar | Shows building-level busyness, not room-level. Often inaccurate for campus buildings. |

---

## Indirect Competitors

| Solution | Type | Mentioned By | What Users Said | How It Relates |
|----------|------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Off-campus cafes | Physical | P06, P09 | "I just go to a cafe. At least I know there will be a table." | Solves the availability problem by leaving campus entirely. Shows willingness to spend money (coffee purchase) to guarantee a space. |
| Library Instagram account | Social media | P08 | "They post photos sometimes. You can kind of tell if it is full." | Provides a rough visual estimate of crowding. Not designed for this purpose but repurposed by students. |

---

## Manual Workarounds

| Workaround | Mentioned By | What They Do | Time/Effort Cost | Why They Tolerate It |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Text friends on campus | P04, P08, P10 | Send WhatsApp messages asking if specific rooms or areas are free | 5 to 10 minutes per attempt, depends on response time | "It is the fastest way to get a real answer. Faster than walking." (P04) |
| Arrive 30 min early | P02, P05 | Come to campus well before their gap to claim a spot | 30+ minutes of extra commuting/waiting | "It is the only guaranteed way. I just accept it." (P02) |
| Claim spots with belongings | P05, P07 | Leave a bag or jacket on a chair while attending class | Risk of items being moved, social friction | "Everyone does it. It is not ideal but it works most of the time." (P05) |
| WhatsApp availability group | P10 | Student-run group where people post when they leave the library | Requires active participation, coverage is inconsistent | "It only works if people actually post. Most days, nobody does." (P10) |
| Default to dorm room | P05, P09 | Give up on finding campus space and study at home | Less effective study environment (roommate, bed, distractions) | "It is not great but at least I stop wasting time searching." (P05) |

---

## "Do Nothing" Behavior

| Behavior | Mentioned By | Why They Gave Up |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Study in hallways or stairwells | P09 | "I sat on the floor in the hallway because I ran out of time to keep looking. It was embarrassing but I had a deadline." |
| Skip studying during gaps | P03 | "Sometimes I just scroll my phone for the 90 minutes because I do not feel like playing the room-finding game again." |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Most common current solution:** Texting friends or arriving early. Both are social/behavioral workarounds, not technological solutions.
2. **Biggest gap:** No real-time, room-level availability information exists. The university portal handles scheduling, not live occupancy.
3. **Strongest workaround signal:** The student-run WhatsApp group (P10) shows clear demand for crowdsourced availability data. The fact that students built this themselves is the strongest evidence of unmet need.
4. **Our opportunity:** A lightweight, real-time room availability tool that requires no advance booking and updates based on actual occupancy. The WhatsApp group proves the concept works; we can make it reliable and scalable.
