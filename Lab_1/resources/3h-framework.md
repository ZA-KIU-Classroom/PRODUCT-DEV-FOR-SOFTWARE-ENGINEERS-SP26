# The 3H Brainstorming Framework

**Course:** CS-PD-2026  
**Used in:** Lab 1  

---

## What It Is

3H is a structured framework for generating problem statements that are grounded in real, observed human experience. The three H's stand for Hairband, Headache, and Heartburn. Each H points to a different source of problem discovery.

The framework exists to prevent the most common failure mode in early-stage product work: generating problems that sound plausible and commercially interesting but that no one has actually experienced. These problems lead to products that are logical, well-built, and completely unused.

---

## The Three H's

### Hairband

**Definition:** A problem in your own daily life that you personally live with, work around, or tolerate.

**The bar:** Low. This does not need to be a world-changing problem. It needs to be real and yours.

**Why this H matters:** Founders who build products around their own lived problems have a natural advantage. They can be their own first user. They know the problem viscerally, not analytically. Some of the best products in the world started as a founder solving their own annoyance.

**Examples of Hairband problems:**
- "I (a university student who commutes) waste 15-20 minutes every morning figuring out which bus route is actually running because the official app shows schedules that are not accurate in real time."
- "I (a freelance developer) spend more than an hour at the end of every project month manually recreating timesheets from commit history and Slack messages because I do not log time as I work."
- "I (a non-native English speaker at an international university) frequently misread the emotional tone of emails from professors because I cannot reliably tell what is polite firmness and what is actual displeasure."

**Common Hairband mistakes:**
- Writing a problem that happened once instead of a recurring pattern.
- Describing a preference rather than a problem: "I wish Spotify had better shuffle" is a preference; "I spend 5 minutes every commute skipping songs because my queue algorithm serves me tracks I have already heard in the same week" is a problem.
- Picking a problem you have because you use a bad tool, not because the underlying task is hard. If the problem disappears when you switch apps, it may not be generalisable.

---

### Headache

**Definition:** A problem you have directly observed in someone else. You witnessed the frustration firsthand. You were present.

**The bar:** You must be able to describe a specific person, in a specific situation, experiencing a specific difficulty. "People have trouble with X" is not a Headache. "My cousin who runs a small restaurant in Tbilisi loses about 30 minutes every Monday morning reconciling last week's cash sales because she cannot trust her part-time staff to record them consistently" is a Headache.

**Why this H matters:** Direct observation is the most reliable form of non-interview discovery. You have not heard about the problem secondhand. You have not extrapolated it from a trend article. You saw it.

**Examples of Headache problems:**
- "My mother (a retired teacher who now tutors privately) spends 40+ minutes per student per month chasing payment because she feels socially awkward sending formal invoices to families she knows personally."
- "A classmate who is a part-time barista has had her shift schedule changed 4 times this semester with less than 18 hours notice each time because her manager uses a group WhatsApp that she sometimes misses."
- "My flatmate (an international student from Kazakhstan) re-enters his bank card details on every new Georgian service website because card-saving is not reliably trusted or available in his experience."

**Common Headache mistakes:**
- Describing something a person complained about verbally without you having actually seen the problem in context. Complaints are not observations.
- Using "people I know" as a category when you can only actually name one person. One person is a Hairband range; a category of people is a Heartburn.
- Projecting a problem onto someone rather than observing it: "My parents probably struggle with online banking" is not an observation unless you have watched them struggle.

---

### Heartburn

**Definition:** A recurring problem that affects a recognisable group of people in a community or context you understand well. You have enough familiarity with this context to know this is not a one-off event.

**The bar:** You must be able to name the community or context and describe your connection to it. "People in general" is not a context. "Third-year students at KIU who are enrolled in three or more project-based courses simultaneously" is a context you might have genuine knowledge of.

**Why this H matters:** Heartburn problems are often where the largest opportunities are, because they affect many people. But they are also the category most vulnerable to fabrication. "Healthcare is inefficient" is not a Heartburn problem you personally know -- it is a theme from a conference talk. The 3H framework forces you to articulate your actual connection to the context.

**Examples of Heartburn problems:**
- "Georgian small business owners who operate both online and in a physical shop consistently lose sold inventory data because local POS systems do not sync with their online store platform, causing them to oversell items they no longer have in stock." [Grounded in: a student who worked part-time at a family business and saw this monthly]
- "Non-native English speaking students in international university programs routinely miss nuance in written academic feedback because feedback is given in formal English that signals register differently from what they learned." [Grounded in: personal experience and multiple conversations with peers from the same background]
- "Junior developers in their first job who are reviewing pull requests for the first time are afraid to leave critical feedback because they have no mental model for what tone is professionally acceptable in code review." [Grounded in: experience from a summer internship and conversations with two other interns in the same program]

**Common Heartburn mistakes:**
- Describing a systemic problem from a macro perspective with no personal grounding: "The educational system does not prepare students for the workforce." This may be true but you are not the person to solve it without far more specific grounding.
- Confusing industry knowledge with personal knowledge. Reading about a problem in the fintech sector does not mean you have the kind of embedded understanding of that context that makes discovery work credible.
- Picking a Heartburn problem because it sounds big rather than because you have genuine insight into it.

## The Quality Test

Before you submit any problem statement, run it through this test:

**Can you answer "where were you when you observed this?"**

If yes, the problem has a chance of being real.  
If no, the problem is probably hypothetical.

A hypothetical problem is one you constructed logically rather than discovered observationally. Hypothetical problems are not worthless -- they sometimes lead to real ones. But they are not ready to be a Lab 1 submission.

---

## Format Reference

Every problem statement in `all-problem-statements.md` should follow this structure:

> **WHO** has trouble with **WHAT**, **WHEN or WHERE**, and it matters because **WHY**.

**WHO** = a specific segment, not a broad category  
**WHAT** = a specific behaviour, failure mode, or unmet need -- not an abstract concept  
**WHEN or WHERE** = the context in which the problem occurs  
**WHY** = the consequence to the person -- the thing that makes this more than a minor annoyance  

---

## What Happens to Your Problems After Lab 1

Your 3 problems are not judged or ranked during Lab 1. They are collected alongside every other team member's problems in a shared pool. In Lab 2 your team applies the Four Filters framework to this pool and selects one problem to pursue. The filters are:

1. Is the problem real? (Is there evidence of actual experience, not assumption?)
2. Is the problem frequent? (Does it happen regularly, or is it a one-time event?)
3. Does the problem cause significant pain? (Is the consequence meaningful enough that someone would pay or change behaviour to solve it?)
4. Can we reach the people who have it? (Do we have access to potential users for discovery interviews?)

A problem that scores well on all four filters is worth pursuing. Your 3H brainstorm is the raw material that makes the Four Filters conversation productive.
