### Ana Beridze

#### H1 -- Hairband

**Problem:**  
International students at Georgian universities who receive written feedback from professors in English spend 20-40 minutes per piece of feedback re-reading it multiple times and searching for tone indicators because they cannot reliably distinguish between "firm but acceptable" feedback and "the professor is genuinely concerned about my progress."

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I experience this myself. I have received feedback emails that I read as harsh and spent three days anxious about them, then showed to a native English speaker who told me the professor was simply being direct and the feedback was actually positive overall. This has happened at least four times this semester.

**Rough severity estimate:** Medium  
The problem does not cause academic failure on its own, but it creates persistent low-level anxiety around written communication that affects how I approach assignments and office hours.

---

#### H2 -- Headache

**Problem:**  
Part-time tutors who teach secondary school students privately in Tbilisi and manage 8-15 students per week lose 30-45 minutes per student per month chasing payment because they feel socially awkward sending formal invoices to families they know personally, leading them to rely on informal reminders that are often ignored or deferred.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
My cousin tutors mathematics privately and I have watched her spend a Sunday evening writing individual WhatsApp messages to 12 families asking about payment, apologising for asking, and rewording each message multiple times to avoid sounding demanding. She told me she is owed approximately 600 GEL from three families who have been deferring for more than 6 weeks.

**Rough severity estimate:** High  
This is a recurring cash flow problem. 600 GEL outstanding represents more than a month of typical tutoring income for her situation.

---

#### H3 -- Heartburn

**Problem:**  
Non-native English speaking students at international universities in Georgia who are in their first or second year routinely interpret written academic feedback as more negative or more critical than the professor intended, because the register and hedging conventions of academic English do not match the directness patterns they learned in English classes designed for general communication.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I have spoken to at least eight classmates from Azerbaijan, Armenia, and Kazakhstan who have independently described the same experience: reading feedback and feeling criticised, then either avoiding office hours (to avoid further criticism) or over-revising work that was already acceptable. Three of them have mentioned avoiding submitting optional work because of uncertainty about how feedback would read.

**Rough severity estimate:** High  
Avoidance of office hours and optional submissions directly affects academic performance and integration into the university environment.

### Giorgi Kvaratskhelia

#### H1 -- Hairband

**Problem:**  
Freelance developers who invoice clients in multiple currencies spend 1-2 hours per invoice cycle recreating formatted invoices from scratch in Google Docs because the invoicing tools they have tried either do not support the GEL/USD split common in Georgian freelance work, or cost more per month than the time they would save.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I do occasional freelance work building WordPress sites and I have invoiced 6 clients this academic year. Every invoice I produce, I open the previous one, save a new version, manually update the exchange rate (which I check on a different website), and reformat the totals. The last invoice took me 45 minutes because the client wanted a GEL total and I had originally quoted in USD.

**Rough severity estimate:** Medium  
The time cost is annoying rather than catastrophic, but the unpredictability of how long it takes means I always defer invoicing until the last moment, which delays my payment.

---

#### H2 -- Headache

**Problem:**  
Junior developers in their first software engineering job who are asked to review pull requests for the first time spend 15-25 minutes per review deciding what tone to use in their comments because they have no mental model for what level of directness is professionally acceptable among peers in a code review context.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I completed a two-month internship last summer at a small development company in Tbilisi. Two other interns joined at the same time as me. We were all asked to review each other's pull requests in the second week. All three of us separately described the same anxiety in our lunch conversations: we would write a comment, decide it sounded too harsh, delete it, rewrite it as a question, decide it was now unclear, and repeat the cycle. One intern avoided leaving any substantive comments for the first three weeks because of this uncertainty.

**Rough severity estimate:** Medium  
The immediate consequence is delayed code reviews. The longer-term consequence is interns and junior developers who avoid giving honest feedback, which degrades the code review process for the whole team.

#### H3 -- Heartburn

**Problem:**  
Small restaurant owners in Tbilisi who take both cash and card payments and have between 1 and 5 staff lose accurate daily sales totals at least 3-4 times per month because their point-of-sale system and their card terminal report in different formats and staff members record the cash separately in a notebook that sometimes goes missing or is filled out incorrectly at the end of a shift.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
My family owns a small Georgian restaurant. My father closes the till every night and I have helped him reconcile daily sales on Sundays for the past two years. He regularly discovers discrepancies of between 30 and 120 GEL that he cannot trace to a specific shift or staff member. He has described this problem to me explicitly as "money that dissolves" and estimates he loses 2-3 hours per week trying to investigate it.

**Rough severity estimate:** High  
Real money is being lost and the cause cannot be identified, which makes it impossible to fix. This has created tension with one staff member who has now left, and my father has considered closing early on weekdays to reduce the exposure.

### Luka Tsiklauri

#### H1 -- Hairband

**Problem:**  
University students who rent apartments for the first time without family help spend 10-15 hours of search time per apartment before finding one to view because listings on Facebook Marketplace lack basic comparative information: floor plans, building floor number, utility inclusion, and accurate neighbourhood descriptions.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I searched for an apartment in Tbilisi for three weeks when I first moved here. I messaged 23 listings, received 14 responses, arranged 8 viewings, and found 2 suitable apartments. The other 13 responses were appointments I cancelled after clarifying questions revealed problems that could have been listed upfront (no heating, ground floor, not the neighbourhood advertised). I have estimated I spent about 14 hours of active search time for this result.

**Rough severity estimate:** Medium  
The time cost is significant and concentrated at a stressful life transition point. Students who find bad apartments because of incomplete listing information and move in anyway face the consequences for an entire academic year.

---

#### H2 -- Headache

**Problem:**  
People who manage shared household expenses among 2-4 flatmates in student housing lose 30-60 minutes per month in awkward conversations about who owes what because the person who pays shared bills tracks the information differently from the people who owe money, creating disputes about amounts that everyone agrees on in principle but cannot verify in practice.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I have three flatmates. One flatmate pays the electricity and internet bill and tracks what each of us owes in a note on their phone that no one else can see. Two months ago there was a dispute about a bill from October that one flatmate claimed had been paid already and our flatmate said had not been. Neither person was lying -- they had simply tracked it in different places and neither record was complete. We spent 45 minutes reconstructing October from individual bank apps and the argument left a bad atmosphere for a week.

**Rough severity estimate:** Medium  
The time cost is moderate. The social cost -- damaged flatmate relationships -- is higher and harder to quantify.

---

#### H3 -- Heartburn

**Problem:**  
KIU students who are enrolled in 3 or more project-based courses simultaneously and work in multiple different project teams across those courses lose 2-3 hours per week context-switching between group chats, task lists, and document repositories because each course has a different collaboration setup (WhatsApp, Telegram, Google Drive, email, GitHub) with no shared convention for naming, version control, or deadline tracking.

**Where this observation comes from:**  
I am currently in this situation: 3 courses with group projects, each using a different communication platform. I have missed one internal team deadline this semester because the reminder was in the wrong chat. I have spoken to at least six other students who describe the same problem, and three of them have developed personal workaround systems (one keeps a master notes page on their phone; another uses calendar alerts for everything; another screenshots all key messages to a dedicated album). All three describe their workaround as "exhausting to maintain."

**Rough severity estimate:** High  
The combination of missed internal deadlines, duplicated effort from context switching, and cognitive load from maintaining multiple incompatible systems directly affects academic performance across all enrolled courses.
