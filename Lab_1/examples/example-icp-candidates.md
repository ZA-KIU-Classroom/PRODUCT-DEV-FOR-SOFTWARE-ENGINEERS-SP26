## Candidate ICP 1

**Linked to problem:** "Non-native English speaking students at international universities in Georgia who are in their first or second year routinely interpret written academic feedback as more negative than intended" (Ana's Heartburn)  
**Original problem author:** Ana Beridze

---

### 1.1 Who Is This Person?

**Segment label:** First and second year international students at English-medium universities in Georgia from Azerbaijani, Armenian, or Central Asian backgrounds

**Demographics (relevant to the problem):**  
Age 18-22. Enrolled in an English-medium degree programme at KIU or a similar institution in Tbilisi or Kutaisi. Native language is not English and not closely related to English. English was studied as a foreign language in a formal classroom setting before university. Has been in Georgia for less than 18 months. Manages coursework independently without significant family guidance on academic culture.

**Psychographics (relevant to the problem):**  
Motivated to perform well but uncertain about academic expectations at the new institution. Values clear communication but is not always sure when it is appropriate to seek clarification from a professor. Tends to interpret ambiguity as negative when under stress. Is building academic English proficiency in parallel with completing degree coursework -- this dual demand creates cognitive load that makes subtle language cues harder to process.

---

### 1.2 Their Context

**Where do they experience this problem?**  
Reading emails from professors or written comments in submitted assignments, typically at home (apartment or student housing) in the evening after a submission. Also occurs when receiving feedback via the university's learning management system.

**When does this problem occur?**  
After any substantial written feedback event: returned assignments, midterm performance reviews, or emails about missed deadlines or submission quality. Frequency increases in the 3-4 weeks before final exams when feedback volume is highest and the student's stress level is elevated.

**How often?**  
2-5 times per semester depending on course structure. More frequent in writing-heavy courses or in courses where the professor communicates primarily by email rather than verbally in class.

---

### 1.3 Their Current Workaround

**What do they do today to cope?**  
Re-read the feedback multiple times, sometimes showing it to a more fluent English speaker (a classmate or language centre staff member) for a "translation" of emotional register. Some students look up specific phrases online to check their typical connotation. A subset simply avoids seeking clarification and treats the feedback as negative regardless.

**Why is the current workaround inadequate?**  
Showing feedback to a third party for interpretation is time-consuming, sometimes embarrassing (requiring the student to admit uncertainty), and not always available. The avoidance response (assuming the worst) leads to real consequences: skipped office hours, reduced engagement with the course, and anxiety that compounds across multiple courses.

---

### 1.4 Why This Segment

**Why is this a promising target?**  
This is a well-defined, reachable group concentrated at a small number of institutions. The problem is frequent, recurrent, and causes measurable academic anxiety. Students in this segment are motivated to solve it because the stakes are their academic performance in a foreign country where they are already navigating significant adjustment challenges.

**What would need to be true for this to be a strong ICP?**  
We need to confirm that the problem is experienced broadly (not just by Ana and the 8 classmates she has spoken to). We also need to understand whether the avoidance behaviour is widespread or whether most students develop functional coping strategies early. And we need to confirm that a digital solution (rather than a social or institutional one) is the right form of intervention.

---

### 1.5 Confidence Assessment

**Confidence:** Medium

**Basis:** Ana has direct personal experience and has spoken to 8 students who describe the same pattern. The problem is grounded in direct observation. However, the sample is small and skewed toward students who are comfortable discussing academic challenges with a peer.

**What interviews would tell us:** Whether the avoidance behaviour is common or isolated; whether students want a technical tool or just more explicit feedback conventions from professors; and whether the problem is specific to written feedback or extends to other written communication in the academic context.

## Candidate ICP 2

**Linked to problem:** "Small restaurant owners in Tbilisi who take both cash and card payments and have 1-5 staff lose accurate daily sales totals 3-4 times per month" (Giorgi's Heartburn)  
**Original problem author:** Giorgi Kvaratskhelia

---

### 2.1 Who Is This Person?

**Segment label:** Owner-operated Georgian restaurant with 1-5 staff and mixed cash/card payments

**Demographics (relevant to the problem):**  
Individual owner or family-owned restaurant in Tbilisi. 1-5 paid staff. Operating for at least 6 months. Processes between 20-80 transactions per day. Accepts both cash and card. Owner is present in the business at least 4 days per week and personally closes the till at least some evenings. Age 30-55 is the likely range but not the defining characteristic -- the defining characteristic is personal operational involvement.

**Psychographics (relevant to the problem):**  
Deeply invested in the business both financially and personally. Technically capable in the context of running a restaurant but not a software user by default -- likely uses WhatsApp as their primary tool for almost everything. Worried about staff theft or error but reluctant to accuse staff without evidence. Finds monthly discrepancy investigation demoralising. Has likely tried at least one approach to fixing the reconciliation problem (changing the notebook format, asking staff to submit photos) and has not found it sustainable.

---

### 2.2 Their Context

**Where do they experience this problem?**  
During end-of-day till close, typically between 22:00 and midnight. Also during the Sunday weekly review when the owner totals the week and discovers weekly discrepancies.

**When does this problem occur?**  
Every 7-10 days on average, more frequently during high-volume periods (weekends, public holidays) when staff transitions between shifts are more chaotic and cash handling volume is higher.

**How often?**  
3-4 incidents per month where the discrepancy is large enough to notice and investigate. Daily minor discrepancies (under 20 GEL) are likely ignored or absorbed.

---

### 2.3 Their Current Workaround

**What do they do today to cope?**  
Some owners cross-reference the card terminal daily report with the cash notebook and POS report manually. Others trust one method over the others and accept a margin of error. Giorgi's father uses Sunday evenings for a full week reconciliation. Staff are sometimes asked to reconstruct what happened during a shift from memory.

**Why is the current workaround inadequate?**  
Manual reconciliation takes 2-3 hours per week in Giorgi's father's case. It cannot identify the cause of the discrepancy -- only the fact that one exists. It does not create a defensible record. And it creates ongoing tension with staff who may feel implicitly suspected.

---

### 2.4 Why This Segment

**Why is this a promising target?**  
Small restaurant owners in Georgia are numerous, underserved by enterprise POS software, and are experiencing a real financial loss that is quantifiable and recurring. The owner is the buyer and the user, simplifying the sales process. The problem has a clear causal chain: mixed payment methods plus paper-based staff recording plus no reconciliation tool equals lost money.

**What would need to be true for this to be a strong ICP?**  
We need to confirm that the problem is common across restaurant owners beyond Giorgi's family. We also need to understand whether owners believe the root cause is staff error, theft, or system fragmentation -- because the framing of a solution would differ significantly. And we need to understand what existing tools they have tried and rejected.

---

### 2.5 Confidence Assessment

**Confidence:** High

**Basis:** Giorgi has two years of direct observation of this problem in a real business context, including specific financial amounts and documented staff consequences. The problem is not a hypothesis -- it has been observed repeatedly.

**What interviews would tell us:** Whether the financial loss magnitude is representative or unusually high; whether the problem is specific to restaurant reconciliation or common across mixed-payment small businesses; and whether owners see a software tool as a plausible solution or whether trust (concern about putting business data in a tool) is a barrier.

## Comparison

| Dimension | Candidate ICP 1: International students | Candidate ICP 2: Restaurant owners |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Problem severity | Medium -- academic anxiety and avoidance behaviour | High -- recurring financial loss and staff tension |
| Reachability for interviews | High -- directly accessible at KIU right now | Medium -- requires outreach through family and personal network |
| Personal connections to segment | Ana has direct experience and 8 peer observations | Giorgi has 2 years of direct observation in one business |
| Workaround cost | Time + emotional | Time + money (2-3 hrs/week + unrecovered GEL losses) |
| Confidence in ICP | Medium | High |

**Team observation:**  
ICP 2 has higher confidence because Giorgi's observation is more direct, the financial pain is quantifiable, and the consequence is clearly severe. ICP 1 has broader reach at KIU because the target users are our classmates. Both are grounded in genuine observation. We will apply Four Filters to the associated problems in Lab 2 to make the decision, rather than choosing based on which ICP feels better defined.
