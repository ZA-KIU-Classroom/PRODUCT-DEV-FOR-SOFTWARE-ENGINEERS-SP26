## Interview Setting

In-person at the library cafe. Participant chose the location. Moderately noisy but participant comfortable. Nino led, Giorgi took written notes.

---

## Verbatim Quotes

**Quote 1**  
Context: asked "Walk me through the last time you needed a quiet place to study and could not find one"

> "I walked into the library at 11, checked all three floors, every seat was taken. Not just kind of full -- actually every single seat. I ended up sitting in the stairwell for an hour before someone left. I have done that twice this semester."

What this tells us: The problem is not that the library is sometimes full -- it is that the participant has no way to know how full it is before arriving. The stairwell detail indicates strong motivation to study there despite the friction.

---

**Quote 2**  
Context: asked "What did you do to try to solve this problem yourself?"

> "I started going at 8am. I do not like mornings. But it was the only way I knew there would be space. Now I basically live here in the mornings just to hold a seat."

What this tells us: The participant has already changed their behavior significantly to work around the problem. Arriving early to hold a seat is a strong signal of pain intensity. This is a current workaround, not just a complaint.

---

**Quote 3**  
Context: asked "Have you ever tried to check availability before leaving home?"

> "I checked the library Instagram once, they had posted something about new furniture. Nothing about seats. There is no way to check. You just show up and hope."

What this tells us: The participant actively looked for a solution and found none. "You just show up and hope" is the core gap. Highly quotable framing of the problem.

---

**Quote 4**  
Context: off-script follow-up -- "If you knew it was full, what would you do instead?"

> "Honestly? I would just go to a cafe. But cafes cost money. If I knew the library was full I would know to budget for it. The worst part is not knowing."

What this tells us: The financial dimension is secondary to the information gap. The pain is uncertainty, not cost. This reframes our problem statement -- the user is not primarily price-sensitive, they are certainty-seeking.

---

## Key Observations

- Participant became visibly animated when describing the stairwell incident -- tone shifted from measured to frustrated
- When asked if they had told library administration about the problem, they said "I never thought to do that" and paused -- suggesting the problem has been normalized
- Participant mentioned a classmate named "Tamara" who has also complained about this -- potential future recruit

---

## Preliminary Insights

- The problem is not insufficient library space -- it is absent real-time information. The library may be empty at 2pm but the participant does not know this.
- Users have developed significant workarounds (arriving at 8am, sitting in stairwells) indicating strong motivation to be at the library specifically.
- The pain is amplified for commuter students because arriving early means a longer commute day, not just an inconvenient schedule change.

---

## JTBD Frame

> "The participant is hiring the '8am arrival habit' to do the job of securing a study seat when they need to study productively on campus. The emotional job is feeling in control of their study day. The social job is performing as a serious, prepared student."

---

## Open Questions

- How many seats does the library actually have? Is it objectively full or does it feel full because popular sections fill first?
- Do non-commuter students experience this pain at the same intensity?
- What would participants consider an acceptable alternative to the library? Does it have to be on campus?

## Interviewer Reflection

Most important learning: The problem is specifically about information asymmetry at the decision point (should I commute today?), not about library capacity.

What to do differently: Giorgi was typing notes on a laptop and the participant glanced at the screen twice. Next time, handwrite notes or angle the screen away.
