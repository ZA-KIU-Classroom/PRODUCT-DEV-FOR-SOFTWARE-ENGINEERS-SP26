## Clusters

### Cluster 1: Students do not know whether a space is available before deciding to commute

Sticky count: 12  
Representative quotes:
- "You just show up and hope." (Interview 2, AR)
- "I've turned around at the bus stop three times this semester. Just went home." (Interview 4, GN)

What this cluster suggests: The core problem is a decision-time information gap, not a library capacity problem. Users need occupancy information at the point of deciding whether to travel, not after they arrive.

---

### Cluster 2: Students have already invented workarounds that cost them significant time or money

Sticky count: 9  
Representative quotes:
- "I started going at 8am. I do not like mornings." (Interview 2, AR)
- "I WhatsApp my friend to check if there is space and ask her to save me a seat. It works maybe half the time." (Interview 1, LT)

What this cluster suggests: Users are already investing real effort to work around the problem. Workarounds are the strongest evidence of pain. The WhatsApp seat-saving behavior hints at a social dimension we had not considered.

---

### Cluster 3: Commuter students feel the pain more intensely than resident students

Sticky count: 7  
Representative quotes:
- "If I knew it was full I would not have spent 40 minutes on the bus." (Interview 3, BK)
- "Students who live on campus can just check and come back. I cannot do that." (Interview 4, GN)

What this cluster suggests: Our ICP is validated. Commuter students experience the same information gap but with asymmetrically higher cost.

---

### Cluster 4: The library does not currently broadcast occupancy information

Sticky count: 4  
Representative quotes:
- "I checked the library Instagram once... nothing about seats." (Interview 2, AR)
- "I do not think anyone is tracking this. No sensors, no staff counting." (Interview 1, LT)

What this cluster suggests: There is no existing system for tracking or broadcasting occupancy. This is either a large opportunity or a large constraint -- requires a conversation with library administration.

---

### Cluster 5 (unexpected): Students are also uncertain about noise levels, not just seat availability

Sticky count: 2  
Representative quotes:
- "Even if there are seats, sometimes it is just too loud to concentrate. I wish I knew that before going." (Interview 3, BK)

What this cluster suggests: Not in our original framing. May expand the scope or may be a secondary pain out of scope for v1. Probe in future interviews but do not expand our problem statement based on 2 stickies.

---

## Pattern Analysis

**Largest cluster:** Cluster 1 (12 stickies). The concentration validates that the core problem is information availability at decision time, not library capacity. We should stop trying to solve the capacity problem and focus on the information layer.

**Most emotionally charged cluster:** Cluster 2. Workarounds contained the most emotionally specific language -- waking up early, failed seat-saving. This is where the pain is felt most viscerally.

**Unexpected cluster:** Cluster 5 (noise levels). We had not considered this dimension. Probably out of scope for MVP but worth one question in script v2.

**Cluster that contradicts original hypothesis:** We originally assumed the problem was "not enough seats." Cluster 1 contradicts this -- the problem is not shortage, it is uncertainty. Users are willing to accept a full library if they knew in advance. They are not willing to travel and discover it on arrival.

---

## Preliminary Problem Statement (draft)

> Commuter students at KIU struggle to decide whether to travel to campus because they have no way to check library occupancy before leaving home, which results in wasted commute time and disrupted study plans.

---

## What We Need More Data On

1. Does this problem exist in other campus study spaces (classrooms used informally, student lounges) or only the library?
2. Is the noise dimension (Cluster 5) worth including in the problem scope or is it a distraction?
3. What would library administration need from us to allow any kind of data collection?
