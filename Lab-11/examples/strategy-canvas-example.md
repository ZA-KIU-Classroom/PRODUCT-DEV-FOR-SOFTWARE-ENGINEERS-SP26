## Blue Ocean Narrative

**What we stopped competing on:**

We made a deliberate choice to reduce our investment in institutional integration. Every university-facing competitor (the university app, the library booking system) has deep institutional integration as their primary strength. Competing on that dimension requires navigating university IT approval processes, formal partnership agreements with long lead times, and product decisions driven by institutional procurement rather than student needs. For a student product at our stage, with a direct-to-student distribution model, deep institutional integration is a resource drain that would slow our iteration speed without producing proportional user value. We compete on student-first data quality instead.

**What we introduced:**

We introduced two dimensions that no competitor currently offers. The first is community-sourced real-time occupancy data: users confirm or update the current status of spaces they visit, creating a living dataset that improves with every contribution. The second is structured amenity tagging: noise level, available power sockets, natural light, proximity to a coffee source. These are the dimensions our users consistently named in interviews as the information they wished they had when searching for a study space, and they are the dimensions that zero existing tools provide. Both dimensions are impossible for a tool designed for general audiences (Google Maps) to implement for the campus context specifically, and they are a lower priority for institutional tools (library systems) designed for formal space management rather than informal discovery.

**Why this combination is right for our specific users:**

Our primary users are first and second-year undergraduates who are still building their understanding of campus geography. They have higher time pressure than postgraduates (shorter gaps between classes), higher uncertainty about which spaces are available, and lower social capital to draw on (they do not yet have established coursemate networks who share space tips informally). The combination of real-time occupancy data, noise tagging, and personalised history is specifically valuable for users who are time-constrained, geography-uncertain, and making repeated independent decisions about where to work. It is not a general-purpose improvement. It is a targeted response to the specific frustrations that this user segment experiences most acutely.
