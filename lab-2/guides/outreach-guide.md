# Guide: Interview Outreach

## Why Outreach Is Hard and Why You Have to Do It Anyway

Most people find it uncomfortable to ask strangers for 30 minutes of their time. That discomfort is normal. It does not go away — experienced product managers feel it too. What changes with experience is the recognition that the worst realistic outcome (someone says no or does not reply) is far less bad than the alternative (building a product based on assumptions).

You need 10 completed interviews by the end of Week 4. That means you need to start outreach this week, before Wednesday 25 March. People are busy. Calendars fill up. Cancellations happen. Schedule more interviews than you think you need.

---

## Who to Interview

Interview people who match your ICP. Do not interview people who match your team.

The most common mistake in this course is interviewing friends who are similar to you and share your assumptions. These interviews feel easy and produce data that confirms what you already believe. They are nearly worthless.

Before scheduling any interview, ask: does this person match the segment, context, and pain dimensions of my ICP? If you are not sure, use your screener questions.

It is better to interview 8 people who strongly match your ICP than 15 people who vaguely match it.

---

## Where to Find Interviewees

### Tier 1: Warm network (start here)

People you already know who match your ICP. This is your fastest and most reliable source. Each team member should identify 3-5 people from their existing network before the end of Lab 2.

Do not overlook people you see regularly but do not know well — classmates you sit near, people you recognise from a club or activity, neighbours. These are warm enough to approach directly.

### Tier 2: Friend of a friend (referrals)

At the end of every interview, ask for a referral: "Is there anyone else you know who deals with this kind of thing? Would you be okay to introduce me?" Referrals are often your best interviews because the social context creates trust before you even start.

Start collecting referrals from your first interview. By your fifth interview, referrals should be your primary source.

### Tier 3: Communities (cold outreach)

Groups, forums, or spaces where people matching your ICP congregate. Examples:

- Facebook or Telegram groups for your target community
- Subreddits or Discord servers relevant to your problem space
- Physical spaces: the corridor your ICP frequents, the library at the time of day they are there, the cafeteria during peak hours

Cold outreach has lower response rates than warm outreach. For every 10 cold messages you send, expect 2-4 responses. Send more than you think you need.

## How to Write an Outreach Message

The goal of an outreach message is to get a reply, not to explain your research in detail. Keep it short. Make it easy to say yes.

### Four elements of a good outreach message

1. **Who you are** — brief, one sentence
2. **What you are researching** — vague enough to not bias the interview, specific enough to screen for ICP match
3. **What you are asking for** — be precise about the time commitment
4. **A low-friction yes** — make it easy to respond

### Template A: Warm contact (friend, classmate, acquaintance)

"Hey [Name], I'm working on a product development project this semester and I'm researching [general topic area — do not name your solution]. I think your experience with [ICP-relevant context] would be really useful. Would you have 20-30 minutes sometime this week to chat? Coffee on me if you're on campus, or happy to do a quick Google Meet."

### Template B: Warm introduction via referral

"Hi [Name], [Referrer] suggested I reach out. I'm doing research for a university project on [general topic] and they thought your experience might be relevant. I'm looking to have a few short conversations with people who [ICP description in plain language]. Would you be open to a 20-30 minute chat this week? Happy to fit around your schedule."

### Template C: Cold outreach to a group or community

"Hi everyone, I'm a [year]-year student at [University] working on a project about [general topic]. I'm looking to speak with people who [ICP context — specific but not solution-revealing]. It would take 20-30 minutes over Google Meet or in person. No selling, no pitching — I just want to understand the experience. Reply here or DM me if you'd be interested. Thanks."

## Scheduling the Interview

Propose specific times. "Let me know when you're free" sounds easy but creates work for the other person. Instead: "Are you free Thursday afternoon between 14:00 and 17:00? Or I could do Friday morning before noon."

Confirm the meeting with a message the day before. People forget. A brief reminder reduces no-shows dramatically.

If the interviewee cancels: reschedule immediately in the same message. "No problem at all — when's a better time for you?" Do not wait for them to come back to you.

---

## Format Options

| Format | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| In-person | Higher quality rapport. Easier to read body language. | Harder to record. Scheduling more complex. |
| Google Meet | Easy to schedule and record (with permission). Works across any location. | Slightly less natural for some people. |
| Phone call | Very informal — some people open up more. Fast to arrange. | Hard to take notes and talk simultaneously. Have a note-taker on the line. |

Avoid interviews over WhatsApp text or email. The back-and-forth destroys conversation flow and makes the Five Whys technique nearly impossible.

---

## During the Interview: Practical Reminders

Ask for recording permission before you start. "Do you mind if I record this so I can focus on listening rather than taking notes?" Most people say yes. If they say no, take detailed notes by hand or have a second team member join to take notes silently.

If two team members attend an interview, agree in advance who leads and who takes notes. Do not switch mid-interview. The note-taker should not speak unless the lead misses something important.

Send a thank-you message within 24 hours. Include a brief genuine observation from the interview: "Really appreciated what you said about the WhatsApp workaround — that was something I hadn't expected." This builds goodwill and makes referral requests more likely to succeed.

---

## Managing Rejection

A non-response after one message is not a rejection. Follow up once after 48 hours with a brief nudge: "Just checking back in on this — still keen to chat if you have 20 minutes this week. Happy to do it whenever is easiest for you."

If they decline or do not respond after a follow-up, move on. Do not message more than twice to the same person.

Track your outreach attempts in the interview tracker. A record of who you contacted, when, and what happened is a useful piece of your Lab 2 homework submission.

---

## Interview Volume Strategy

Do not interview only people who are easy to reach. Vary your sources.

A good interview pool for Checkpoint 1 should include:
- At least 3 people you know personally
- At least 3 people you reached through referral
- At least 2 people you reached through cold outreach

This variation tests whether your ICP generalises beyond your immediate network, which is an important validation signal. If the problem only shows up for people you already know, that is important information.
