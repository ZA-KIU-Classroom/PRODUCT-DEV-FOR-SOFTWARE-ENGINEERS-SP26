# Guide: Building Your Ideal Customer Profile

## What the ICP Is and Is Not

An Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) is a precise description of the specific type of person who experiences your problem most acutely and is therefore the most valuable person to interview and, eventually, to serve.

It is not a demographic summary. It is not "18-25 year old university students." It is not a persona with a made-up name and a stock photo.

It is a functional description of a real human's context, behaviour, pain, and motivation — specific enough that you could walk into a room and identify that person within a few minutes of conversation.

---

## Why Precision Matters

Imprecise ICPs lead to imprecise interviews, which lead to imprecise problem statements, which lead to products that nobody buys.

**Imprecise:** University students who have trouble studying.

**Precise:** Second-year and third-year Computer Science students at KIU who live more than 30 minutes from campus by public transport, have three or more hours of gap time between classes per week, and currently spend that time in corridors or the cafeteria because every quiet room is either occupied or requires booking through a system they find unreliable.

The precise version tells you exactly who to call, what to ask them, and how to recognise whether your product is solving their problem.

---

## The Two-ICP Structure

You are building two ICPs in Lab 2:

- **Primary ICP:** The person who experiences your committed problem most acutely. This is your main interview target.
- **Secondary ICP:** The person who experiences your second-ranked problem candidate most acutely. You keep this alive until interview evidence confirms or disconfirms your primary commitment.

Both ICPs must be documented in `00-foundation/team-icp.md` by your homework deadline.

## The Eight ICP Dimensions

Build your ICP by filling in all eight of these dimensions. Shallow answers to any dimension will cost you interview quality.

### 1. Segment

Who is this person in observable terms? Not "a student" but a specific type of student. Include role, institution, year, context. The goal is that someone reading your segment description could immediately think of a real person who matches.

**Weak:** University students
**Strong:** Second and third-year Computer Science students at a Georgian university who commute from outside the city

### 2. Context

When and where does the problem occur? What is the situation the person is in when the pain is felt? Be precise about the physical and temporal environment.

**Weak:** When they are on campus
**Strong:** During two- to three-hour gaps between scheduled classes, when they are already on campus and cannot justify travelling home, but have no reliable quiet workspace available

### 3. Primary Pain

What is the specific experience of discomfort? What exactly goes wrong? Do not describe the cause — describe the felt experience.

**Weak:** They cannot find a study space
**Strong:** They spend 20-40 minutes of each gap period walking between rooms and floors looking for a free quiet space, then settle for a noisy environment that breaks their concentration, and end the day feeling they wasted time they intended to use productively

### 4. Current Workaround

What are they already doing to cope with this problem? This is the most important dimension for validation. If they have no workaround, the pain may not be acute enough.

**Weak:** Nothing, they just deal with it
**Strong:** They message three different WhatsApp groups asking if anyone has a spot, check the library booking system (which only shows reservations for full rooms, not individual seats), and often end up in the cafeteria with AirPods on trying to block out noise

### 5. Job to Be Done

What outcome is the person actually trying to achieve? Use the JTBD framing: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]." The product is a means to an end. What is the end?

**Weak:** Find a study space
**Strong:** When I have a gap between classes, I want to immediately know where I can go to do focused work, so I can use that time productively instead of losing it to searching and settling

### 6. Success Signal

How would this person know the problem is solved? What would their behaviour or feeling change to if your solution worked? This becomes a key question in your interviews.

**Weak:** They find a room
**Strong:** They sit down within 5 minutes of arriving on campus and work without interruption for the duration of their gap. They stop checking WhatsApp groups to find seats. They report feeling less stressed about unproductive days.

### 7. Where to Find Them

Where do people matching this ICP actually spend time? This is your outreach strategy. Be specific enough that you could execute this today.

**Weak:** On campus
**Strong:** In the corridor outside Room 214 between 10:00 and 12:00 on Tuesdays and Thursdays. In the CS department lounge. In the Computer Science first-floor lab during off-hours. In the KIU Student Resources Facebook group.

### 8. Why You, Why Now

What makes this person approachable and motivated to talk to you? What gives you access or credibility? Why would they give you 30 minutes of their time?

**Weak:** We are also students
**Strong:** Three team members are in the CS programme and personally know students matching this profile. We can schedule interviews through existing friendships and shared classes. The problem affects us directly, which gives the interview a peer-to-peer quality that puts interviewees at ease.

## Common ICP Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Making it too broad to be useful**
If your ICP could describe half the student population, it is not an ICP. Narrow it until you could identify 20-50 specific real people who match it.

**Mistake 2: Building it entirely from assumptions**
Your first ICP is necessarily based on what you know before interviews. That is fine. But be honest about what is observed versus assumed. Your ICP will evolve as you conduct interviews.

**Mistake 3: Confusing demographics with context**
Age, gender, and study year are not the ICP. They are surface descriptors. The ICP is about the specific situation in which the problem occurs and the specific behaviour the person exhibits.

**Mistake 4: Building one ICP and calling it done**
You need two ICPs in Lab 2 — one for your committed problem and one for your second-ranked candidate. Keep both alive until interview evidence tells you to drop one.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting the workaround**
The current workaround dimension is the single most important thing to nail before interviews. If you cannot describe the workaround, you have not done enough thinking about the problem yet.

---

## Updating Your ICP After Interviews

Your first ICP is a hypothesis. Every interview will give you new information. After each interview, ask:

- Did this person match our ICP?
- What details were wrong about our ICP?
- Who else did they mention who also has this problem?
- Should we narrow or broaden the segment?

Document ICP updates in your interview logs and carry them forward into a revised ICP document as needed. By Checkpoint 1 (Week 4) your ICP should be grounded in at least 5 real conversations, not just team assumptions.
