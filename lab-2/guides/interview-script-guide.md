# Guide: Building Your Interview Script

## The Purpose of This Document

Your interview script is not a rigid questionnaire. It is a structured conversation guide. The goal of a customer discovery interview is to learn the truth about how someone experiences a problem — not to confirm your assumptions about it.

This guide covers the principles behind good interview questions, the five-phase structure of a strong interview, and the most common mistakes to avoid.

---

## The Mom Test in Practice

The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick, 2013) gives three rules for questions that produce honest answers:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Never pitch your solution during discovery interviews. The moment you describe what you are building, you corrupt the data.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generalities or opinions about the future.** "Have you ever..." and "Tell me about the last time..." produce real data. "Would you..." and "Do you think you would..." produce fiction.
3. **Listen more than you talk.** Your ratio should be roughly 20% talking, 80% listening. If you are explaining things or defending your idea, you are doing it wrong.

## The Five Phases of a Good Interview

### Phase 1: Opening and Context (5 minutes)

Purpose: Make the interviewee comfortable and establish the topic without revealing your solution.

What you do here:
- Thank them for their time
- Explain that you are researching a problem space, not pitching a product
- Ask them to tell you about their role, context, and daily routine in the relevant area

Example opener questions:
- "Can you tell me a bit about how you typically spend your time on campus between classes?"
- "Walk me through what a typical study day looks like for you."
- "How long have you been doing X, and what does your workflow look like?"

Do not ask anything specific about your problem yet. Let them paint a picture of their world.

### Phase 2: Problem Exploration (10 minutes)

Purpose: Find out whether the problem you suspect exists actually shows up in their life without prompting it.

What you do here:
- Ask broad questions about their experience in the relevant domain
- Listen for mentions of frustration, workarounds, or friction without directing them there
- If they mention friction, dig in immediately with follow-up questions

Example questions:
- "What are the most frustrating parts of that routine for you?"
- "Is there anything in your day that consistently wastes time or causes stress?"
- "Tell me about a time recently when something in that area went wrong."

If they mention your target problem unprompted, that is strong signal. If you have to introduce it, the signal is weaker.

### Phase 3: Problem Deep Dive (10 minutes)

Purpose: Understand the problem with enough depth and specificity to write a meaningful problem statement.

What you do here:
- Ask them to walk you through a specific recent instance of the problem
- Use Five Whys to get to root causes
- Ask about consequences and impact
- Ask about their current workaround

Example questions:
- "Tell me about the last time that happened. Walk me through it step by step."
- "When that happens, what do you do?"
- "Why do you do it that way?"
- "What happens if you don't solve it that way?"
- "How often does this happen?"
- "What's the worst part of it?"

### Phase 4: Current Solutions (5 minutes)

Purpose: Understand what the interviewee is already using or doing to cope, and how satisfied they are with it.

What you do here:
- Ask about existing tools, services, or workarounds
- Ask how well those solutions work
- Ask what is missing from them

Example questions:
- "Have you tried anything to solve this? What did you try?"
- "How well does that work?"
- "What does [the current solution] not do that you wish it would?"
- "Have you paid for anything to help with this? Why or why not?"

### Phase 5: Close (5 minutes)

Purpose: Gather any final insights, get referrals, and leave on good terms.

What you do here:
- Ask a catch-all question
- Ask for referrals to others who might experience the same problem
- Thank them and confirm if you can follow up

Example questions:
- "Is there anything about this area that I haven't asked you about but that you think is important?"
- "Who else do you know who deals with this kind of thing? Would you be comfortable introducing me?"
- "Would it be okay if I followed up with a quick message if something comes up?"

## Writing Mom Test-Compliant Questions

### The Four Rules for Question Writing

**Rule 1: Past tense only**
Bad: "Would you use a product that helped you find study spaces?"
Good: "Tell me about the last time you had trouble finding a place to study."

Bad: "Do you think you'd pay for this?"
Good: "Have you ever paid for anything to help with this problem? What did you pay for?"

**Rule 2: Specific, not general**
Bad: "Do you ever have trouble finding study spaces?"
Good: "Walk me through what happened the last time you had a gap between classes and couldn't find somewhere quiet to work."

**Rule 3: Their life, not your solution**
Bad: "If there was an app that showed you free study rooms in real time, would you use it?"
Good: "What do you do when you can't find a place to study?"

**Rule 4: Open-ended**
Bad: "Is finding a study space stressful?" (Yes/No answer)
Good: "How do you feel when you're struggling to find somewhere to work?" (Open answer)

## Common Interview Mistakes

**Leading questions**
Bad: "Do you find the current booking system frustrating?"
Why it fails: You are suggesting the answer. They will agree even if they have no strong feeling.
Good: "What do you think of the current booking system?"

**Pitching during the interview**
The moment you describe your solution, interviewees switch from honest reflection to polite feedback. They will tell you it sounds great. That is not useful data. Save the pitch for after you have finished gathering information.

**Asking hypothetical future questions**
"Would you pay for...", "Would you switch to...", "Would you recommend..." — these questions measure people's imagination about a future scenario, not their actual behaviour. They are almost always too optimistic. Ask about the past.

**Accepting surface-level answers**
If someone says "it's a bit annoying," that is not enough. Ask "tell me more about that" or "can you walk me through the last time that happened?" Shallow answers produce shallow problem statements.

**Talking more than you listen**
If you are spending more than 30% of the interview talking, you are doing it wrong. Your job is to ask short questions and then be quiet.

**Moving on without the "why"**
When someone mentions a workaround or a frustration, the most important follow-up is "why?" and then "why?" again. Surface behaviours rarely reveal root causes. Root causes are what you need.

## Question Count Requirements

Your interview script v1 must contain a minimum of 10 questions per problem candidate. These should span all five phases. A good breakdown is:

| Phase | Minimum questions |
|-------|------------------|
| Opening and Context | 2 |
| Problem Exploration | 3 |
| Problem Deep Dive | 3 |
| Current Solutions | 2 |
| Close | 1 |

You will not ask all 10 questions in every interview. Some conversations will go deep on phase 3 and skip phase 4. That is fine. The script is a guide, not a script.

---

## Preparing for Your Interviews

Before each interview, review:
- Your ICP: does this person match?
- Your problem statement: what are you trying to learn or challenge?
- Your script: what are the 3-4 most important questions if time is short?

After each interview, within 24 hours:
- Write up your interview log using the template in Lab 3
- Note any surprises, contradictions, or new questions
- Update your ICP if the interviewee did not match your assumptions

---

## A Note on Recording

Always ask permission before recording. If the interviewee declines, take notes by hand. Notes taken during the interview are better than memory reconstructed afterwards. If two team members attend the same interview, one leads the conversation and the other takes notes.
