# Example: Problem Statements — Strong vs Weak

> This document shows the difference between weak and strong problem statements so you understand what the grading rubric is looking for. Do not copy these examples.

---

## The Formula

[Specific user segment] experience [specific pain] when [specific context], causing [specific consequence]. Currently they cope by [specific workaround], which is inadequate because [specific reason].

---

## Example Problem 1: Study Spaces

### Weak Version

"Students at university have trouble finding places to study. This is a common problem and existing solutions don't work well. We want to help students be more productive."

**Why this fails:**
- "Students at university" is not a user segment — it describes half the world
- "Trouble finding places to study" is not specific pain — what exactly is the experience?
- "Existing solutions don't work well" is an assertion without evidence
- "Be more productive" is the desired outcome, not the problem
- There is no workaround described
- There is no consequence described
- Nothing in this statement could not have been written by someone who had never spoken to a student

### Strong Version

"Second-year and third-year Computer Science students at KIU who commute from outside the city experience wasted productive time and elevated stress during 2-3 hour class gaps when they cannot locate an available quiet workspace on campus without spending 20-40 minutes searching. This causes them to fall behind on coursework and leave campus feeling they wasted the day. Currently they cope by messaging multiple WhatsApp groups asking if anyone knows a free spot, checking a library booking portal that only shows full-room reservations and not individual seat availability, and ultimately settling for noisy corridors or the cafeteria. This workaround consumes the very time they are trying to use productively and regularly fails to resolve the core problem."

**Why this works:**
- The user segment is precise: year, programme, commuter status
- The context is precise: 2-3 hour class gaps, already on campus
- The pain is described as an experience: wasted time, elevated stress
- The consequence is specific: falling behind on coursework, feeling the day was wasted
- The workaround is described in detail: WhatsApp groups, the library system, what is wrong with each
- Someone reading this could immediately go find people who match the description and interview them

## Example Problem 2: Code Feedback

### Weak Version

"CS students don't get enough feedback on their code. Office hours are limited and it's frustrating."

**Why this fails:**
- No specific context — when exactly does this happen?
- "Not enough feedback" is vague — what does inadequate feedback look like and cost?
- "Frustrating" is an emotion descriptor, not a consequence
- No workaround described
- Not specific enough to design an interview around

### Strong Version

"First-year and second-year Computer Science students at KIU experience assignment paralysis during the 4-7 days between submitting a question on the course forum and receiving a response from teaching staff, particularly when debugging blocking errors that prevent them from making progress on the rest of an assignment. This causes them to miss self-imposed study schedules, fall behind on multi-part assignments, and submit work they know is suboptimal. Currently they cope by posting on public Stack Overflow threads (which rarely produce relevant answers for course-specific problems), asking classmates who are often equally stuck, and waiting — losing 1-3 hours of productive work per blocking error. None of these approaches reliably unblocks them within the same day."

**Why this works:**
- Specific segment: year, programme
- Specific context: the 4-7 day gap, when debugging blocking errors
- Specific consequence: missed schedules, suboptimal submissions
- Workaround described in detail with its failure mode
- The time cost is quantified: "1-3 hours of productive work per blocking error"

## Checklist: Is Your Problem Statement Strong Enough?

Before submitting, run your statement through this check:

- [ ] Can I identify a real person who matches the user segment right now?
- [ ] Is the context specific enough that I can design an interview question around it?
- [ ] Would someone matching this profile read this statement and say "yes, that is exactly what it feels like"?
- [ ] Is the consequence something that actually matters to this person?
- [ ] Is the workaround described specifically enough that I could replicate it?
- [ ] Is there anything in this statement I invented rather than observed or experienced?

If any answer is no, revise the statement.
