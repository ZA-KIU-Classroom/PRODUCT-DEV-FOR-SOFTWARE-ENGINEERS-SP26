# Example: Interview Script v1

> This is an annotated worked example. Annotations appear as blockquotes after each section. Do not copy this script — your questions must be specific to your problem and your ICP.

**Team Name:** Team Spotlight (example)
**Problem this script is for:** CS/Engineering students at KIU cannot find available quiet study spaces during class gaps, causing significant time loss.
**ICP this script targets:** Primary — second and third-year CS students who commute and have 90-minute+ class gaps
**Version:** v1.0
**Date created:** 19 March 2026

---

## Screener Questions

Before scheduling the interview, confirm the match:

1. "Are you currently in second year or third year CS or Engineering at KIU?"
2. "Do you commute to campus from outside the city centre?"
3. "Do you have gaps of 90 minutes or more between classes most weeks?"

> **Annotation:** Three screener questions is usually enough. You are checking segment, context, and approximate problem proximity — not fishing for the answer. If they say no to question 3 but have gaps of 60-70 minutes, use your judgement.

---

## Interview Introduction

"Thanks for making the time — I really appreciate it. I'm doing research for a university project where we're trying to understand how students use campus during the day, especially the time between classes. I'm not selling anything and I'm not pitching a product — I just want to understand what your experience is like. There are no right or wrong answers, and everything you tell me is just to help our team understand the space. Is that okay?

Do you mind if I take notes while we talk?"

> **Annotation:** Note what is not said here. The interviewer does not say "we're building an app to help you find study spaces." The moment you pitch, the interviewee becomes a reviewer instead of a source of truth. Keep the topic broad and neutral.

---

## Phase 1: Opening and Context (5 minutes)

**Question 1:** "Walk me through what a typical day on campus looks like for you — what time you arrive, what you do, how you move through the day."

> **Annotation:** This is a deliberately open question. It gives the interviewee space to describe their world without directing them toward your hypothesis. You are listening for patterns, friction, and structure. Do not interrupt to guide them toward your topic.

**Question 2:** "What do you usually do between classes? How do you spend that time?"

> **Annotation:** This is your pivot into the relevant domain. Still open — "how do you spend that time" covers everything from productive work to scrolling to sleeping. You want to hear what they naturally do, not what you expect them to do.

**Follow-up prompts ready:**
- "Can you tell me more about that?"
- "How has that been working for you?"
- "Has it always been like that, or has it changed?"

---

## Phase 2: Problem Exploration (10 minutes)

**Question 3:** "What are the most frustrating or time-wasting parts of being on campus during the day?"

> **Annotation:** "Most frustrating or time-wasting" invites them to surface what genuinely bothers them. You are listening to see whether the study space problem comes up unprompted. If it does, this is strong signal. If it does not, note that too — it might mean the problem is not top-of-mind for this person.

**Question 4:** "Is there anything you regularly do on campus that you wish you didn't have to do — any process or routine that feels like wasted effort?"

> **Annotation:** This question is fishing for workaround behaviour without naming the problem. "Anything you wish you didn't have to do" surfaces the workarounds people have normalised. Listen for mentions of searching, checking systems, asking people, settling for suboptimal environments.

**Question 5:** "Tell me about a time recently when your time between classes didn't go the way you'd hoped. What happened?"

> **Annotation:** This is your strongest Phase 2 question. "Tell me about a time recently" is past-tense and specific. "Didn't go the way you'd hoped" is neutral enough to surface any kind of friction, not just study space friction. Wait for the full story before asking follow-up questions.

---

## Phase 3: Problem Deep Dive (10 minutes)

> **Annotation:** You only go into deep dive if the problem has come up — either unprompted in Phase 2 or in response to a gentle introduction. If they have not mentioned anything about spaces or studying during class gaps, you can introduce it: "One area I've been curious about is finding somewhere to work during class gaps — has that ever come up for you?" Then gauge their reaction before going deeper.

**Question 6:** "Walk me through what you actually do when you have a two-hour gap and need to get some work done. What's your process from the moment the previous class ends?"

> **Annotation:** Step-by-step past-tense question. "From the moment the previous class ends" is a very specific anchor. This produces a story you can map onto a timeline. Listen for decision points, alternatives considered, time elapsed, and emotional tone.

**Question 7:** "You mentioned [whatever they described]. Why do you do it that way? What made that your approach?"

> **Annotation:** Always tie follow-up questions to something they actually said. "You mentioned" is the most important phrase in a customer discovery interview. Then ask "why" at least two more times in response to their answer. You are looking for the root cause beneath the behaviour.

**Question 8:** "What happens on a day when that doesn't work — when you can't find what you're looking for? What does that cost you?"

> **Annotation:** "What does that cost you" is the consequence question. You want to understand the downstream impact: time lost, stress experienced, work not completed, feeling at the end of the day. Consequences tell you how painful the problem actually is, which is different from how often it occurs.

---

## Phase 4: Current Solutions (5 minutes)

**Question 9:** "Have you ever tried anything — an app, a system, asking someone — to deal with this? What did you try? How well did it work?"

> **Annotation:** This question surfaces the full workaround inventory. Let them list everything they have tried before you ask follow-up questions about any specific one. Do not interrupt with "oh, what about the library booking system?" — let them bring it up themselves if they use it.

**Question 10:** "If you could change one thing about how this works right now, what would it be?"

> **Annotation:** This is not a solution-design question. "What would you change" is asking them to describe the gap between current and ideal in their own language. Their answer will often tell you more about the JTBD than anything else in the interview. Listen carefully.

---

## Phase 5: Close (5 minutes)

**Question 11 (catch-all):** "Is there anything about studying on campus or using campus time that I haven't asked about but that you think matters?"

**Referral question:** "Is there anyone else you know who deals with this kind of thing regularly? I'd love to talk to them — would you be okay to make an introduction?"

**Close:** "This has been really helpful — I've learned a lot. Thank you. If something comes up that's relevant, is it okay if I send you a quick message?"
